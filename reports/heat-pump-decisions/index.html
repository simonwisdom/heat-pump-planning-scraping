When do heat-pump installs need planning permission?

Most heat pumps don’t need planning permission — they’re allowed automatically as long as they meet certain rules on size, noise, and distance from neighbours (the planning system calls this “permitted development”). The rest do need a planning application, and that’s what this dataset captures.

Sources for the permitted-development rules: Planning Portal — air source and ground/water source heat pumps (England); gov.wales (Wales). The underlying legislation is the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) (England) Order 2015, Sch. 2 (Part 14, Classes G–H). England’s rules were eased on 29 May 2025 (1 m boundary rule removed; size limit raised to 1.5 m³), as part of the Warm Homes Plan (DESNZ, last updated 18 March 2026).

Over time

To gauge the share of installs that need planning permission, we compare planning applications against MCS — the certification scheme that records every heat pump fitted by an accredited installer in the UK, whether or not planning was needed. The chart below puts the two side by side: certified installs in red, planning applications in green.

In 2025 there were 5,675 planning applications for heat pumps versus 61,228 certified installations. So only about 9.3% of installs needed planning permission. The average across 2015–2025 is roughly 10%.

What each application type means
Outline
Establishes whether the principle of a development is acceptable; specific “reserved matters” are settled in a later application.
Amendment
Changes to a granted permission — non-material amendments (s.96A) or variations of conditions (s.73, Town and Country Planning Act 1990).
Full
Detailed proposals for development not covered by permitted-development rights; includes householder works (extensions, conservatories, etc.).
Conditions
Formal approval (discharge) of details required by conditions on a granted permission, including reserved matters after an outline consent.
Heritage
Listed Building Consent for works affecting a building of special architectural or historical interest (Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990).
Other
Non-standard routes (certificates of lawfulness, prior-approval observations, neighbouring-authority notifications, etc.).
Trees
Works to trees protected by a Tree Preservation Order, or s.211 notice for trees in a conservation area.

Definitions taken from the Planning Portal consent-type pages and the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 (s.96A non-material amendments, s.73 variation of conditions, s.211 trees in conservation areas) and the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990.

Edge cases flagged in the proposal

The project proposal called out four groups of applications to look at specifically — listed buildings, flats, wind turbines, and (for the sound-assessment workstream) noise mentions. These groups are picked out by searching application descriptions and addresses for keywords, and each is compared to the 6.6% national average. Listed buildings and conservation areas are actually refused less often than average — these applications tend to come in well prepared, often with extra acoustic or visual mitigation. Applications on flats and the few mentioning wind turbines are refused more often. Wind turbine numbers are small, so treat that figure as a hint rather than a hard finding.

Groups are identified by keywords on the application’s description and address — “listed”, “grade I/II”, or “conservation area” for heritage; “flat”, “apartment”, or “block” for flats; explicit mention for the rest. An application can fall into more than one group. The listed / heritage flag is a superset of the Heritage consent type above — it also catches Full applications that mention a conservation area or listed-building context, which is why the two refusal rates land close together.

Method

Each application’s outcome is taken from the council’s own decision text and grouped into approved, refused, withdrawn, or other categories using a list of keyword rules. Refusal rates are calculated only over applications that have an approval or refusal recorded — withdrawn, pending, and unrecorded outcomes are left out so they don’t distort the rate. Regions are worked out from the application’s postcode; for the small share of records with no postcode, the region is filled in from the council’s other applications (or, for a handful of councils whose records carry no postcode at all, from a manual council→region map).
